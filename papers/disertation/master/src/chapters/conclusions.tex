\chapter{Conclusions}
\label{chapter:conclusions}

There is a lot of potential for quantum computing in computational fluid dynamics.
Any speedup in CFD simulations could have significant impact in many important fields like aerodynamics, meteorology and medicine.
Despite this, the field of Quantum Computational Fluid Dynamics is still in its early stages.

Various groups are working on different approaches to extend classical CFD methods to quantum computers.
The algorithms generally agree with both classical counterparts and theoretical solutions.
That being said, because of the limited number of qubits we can simulate, quantum CFD simulations are limited to low-dimensional, simple problems.
Theoretical improvements to the runtime of simulations have been shown, but all of them have practical drawbacks that need to be addressed.

The focus in recent times has been exactly to address these limitations and make the algorithms viable for near term quantum computers.

In this work we presented a fully generic and parameterized implementation of a QLBM algorithm for the advection-diffusion equation.
We validated the implementation with various experiments of increasing complexity and demonstrated that it matches classical simulation within a reasonable error margin.

We successfully demonstrated a generic method for implementing local, linear, norm-preserving boundary conditions in the QLBM.
The method leverages LCU and SVD to handle non-unitary boundary operators, and optimizes circuit design by combining collision and boundary steps.
Validation through a 1D advection-diffusion simulation with solid walls confirmed that the quantum implementation matches classical results within numerical precision.
