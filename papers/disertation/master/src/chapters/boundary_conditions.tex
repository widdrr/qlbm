\chapter{Boundary Conditions}
\label{chapter:boundary-conditions}

In an LBM simulation, enforcing boundary conditions involves determining unknown incoming distributions at boundary nodes based on known outgoing distributions and other auxiliary information.
Depending on how the boundary is encoded into the lattice, LBM simulators distinguish between
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Wet-Nodes}: The boundary conditions are applied directly on the fluid nodes at the boundary.
    \item \textbf{Link-Wise}: The boundary conditions are applied between fluid nodes and solid nodes.
\end{itemize} \cite{olbug:18}.

Most of the existing literature opts to implement specific link-wise boundary conditions, such as bounce-back, directly in the circuit \cite{ljubomir2022quantum,jennings2026simulating,lee2025advanced}.
This approach benefits from being able to efficiently embed simple conditions directly into the circuit.

For our simulator we wanted to try a fully parametrized approach, able to be customized for a large class of boundary conditions, then evaluate the incured cost of generality.
\section{Linear Boundary Formulation}
\label{sec:bc-formulation}

Due to the linear constraints of quantum operations, we focused on modeling the unknown distributions as linear combinations of the known distributions at the boundary nodes.
This is a well known wet-node approach, detailed by \cite{zou1997pressure}.
Additionally we have chosen to only consider boundary conditions that preserve the total quantity at the boundary node.
Mathematically, this can be expressed as:

\begin{align*}
& f_i^{post}(x) = \sum_j B_{x}^{ij} f_j^{pre}(x) \\
\intertext{or in matrix form}
& \mathbf{f}^{post}(x) = B_x \cdot \mathbf{f}^{pre}(x)
\intertext{with the required quantity conservation property}
& \sum_i f_{i}^{post} = \sum_i f_{i}^{pre}
\end{align*}
where $x$ is the lattice site, $f_i$ the distribution for link $i$ and $B_x$ the boundary condition operator for site x.

From the quantity preservation restriction, it follows that for each $B_x$ all columns sum up to 1, making them left-stochastic matrices.
Each $B_x$ can be embedded in a larger operator $B$, describing all boundary conditions for the entire lattice.
Simplest way to imagine it is to start with a block-diagonal containing all defined $B_x$, with $I$ instead for non-boundary lattices.

\begin{equation}
B = \begin{pmatrix}
    B_0    & 0      & \dots  & \dots  & 0 \\
    0      & B_1    & \ddots &        & \vdots \\
    \vdots & \ddots & I    & \ddots & \vdots \\
    \vdots &        & \ddots & I      & 0 \\
    0      & \dots  & \dots  & 0      & B_{N-1}
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
From the construction we can see it is also stochastic. This is important to ensure the stability of the simulation.


Because of the chosen quantum state encoding, link distributions for a given site are not adjacent in the vector representation of the quantum state; instead all distributions of a given link are adjacent.
The real $B$ matrix will however be a permutation of the block-diagonal variant, meaning all relevant properties are invariant, such as eigenvalues and stochasticity.

\section{Quantum Implementation via LCU and SVD}
\label{sec:bc-implementation}

Since the boundary operator $B$ is generally non-unitary, it cannot be directly implemented as a quantum gate.
To address this, we once again employ the LCU technique.
As established, a diagonal, real-valued operator is straightforward to express as a linear combination of two unitaries:
\begin{equation*}
C = \frac{1}{2} (C + i\sqrt{I - C^2}) + \frac{1}{2}(C - i\sqrt{I - C^2})
\end{equation*}

The only issue is that $B$ is not diagonal.
Thankfully, we can perform Singular Value Decomposition (SVD):
\begin{equation}
    B = U \Sigma V^{\dagger}
\end{equation}
where $U, V$ are unitary and $\Sigma$ is diagonal.
Critically, the SVD algorithm ensures $\Sigma$ will also be real-valued (positive semidefinite in fact).
Using these facts, $\Sigma$ can be decomposed as a linear combination of two unitaries, and $U$ and $V^{\dagger}$ distributed to obtain a linear combination for $B$:
\begin{equation}
    B = \frac{1}{2} (U (\Sigma + i\sqrt{I - \Sigma^2}) V^{\dagger} + U (\Sigma - i\sqrt{I - \Sigma^2}) V^{\dagger})
\end{equation}

Note however that unlike with the collision operator we do not have any guarantees $\Sigma$ will be contractive.
To solve this, we took the simplest approach possible and normalized $\Sigma$ via dividing by its spectral norm.
This scaling is irelevant to our overall algorithm, thanks to the renormalization performed at the end of each simulation step.

Lastly, boundary nodes should be exempt from the normal collision operator. In a way, the boundary condition can be thought of as a special collision operator for that node.
Mathematically, this translates in replacing the corresponding entries in the collision operator with identities.
\section{Pre-Streaming Boundary Conditions}
\label{sec:bc-optimization}

If we were to use the standard wet node scheme of performing collision followed by streaming and lastly apply boundary conditions, we would have to perform two independent LCU circuits.
The consequences of such an operation would be a decrease in the success probability of the entire circuit, since we would need each independent LCU to succeed.

To solve this, we decided to apply the boundary conditions \emph{before} streaming the distributions.
Now the two operators are consecutive and can be replaced with a single combined operator, which will be decomposed via SVD and applied with a single LCU.


It is straightforward to prove equivalence with post-streaming boundary conditions, in fact every post-streaming boundary condition operator $B_{post}$ can be converted to a $B_{pre}$ via linear transformations.
Begin by considering a single iteration of each circuit:
\begin{align*}
    U_{pre} = S B_{pre} C \\
    U_{post} = B_{post} S C
\end{align*}

We want these iterations to be equivalent, so we will force equality:
\begin{align*}
    U_{pre} &= U_{post} \\
    S B_{pre} C &= B_{post} S C \\
\intertext{Right-multiplying by $C^{-1}$ to drop the common operator:}
    S B_{pre} &= B_{post} S \\
\intertext{Left-multiplying by $S^{\dagger}$:}
    B_{pre} &= S^{\dagger} B_{post} S
\end{align*}
Intuitively, this is like streaming ahead, applying the post-streaming boundary conditions and streaming back.

\subsection{Probability Analysis}

Merging the two operations into a single LCU is only worthwhile if the success probability is higher than the separate approach.
We will both prove this fact mathematically and perform a numerical experiments to quantify the probability gain.
For the purpose of this analysis, the streaming operator is ignored, replaced by the identity.

Recall the probability of measuring the correct LCU substate from Formula~{\ref{eq:lcu_prob}}.

While not required for correctness, we can normalize the $C$ operator as well, obtaining $\tilde{C} = C / \|C\|_2$.
Due to the classical renormalization we perform, it increases the success probability of the LCU.

If $\ket{\psi}$ is the initial state, then probability of success for the $C$ operator is:
\begin{equation*}
    P_C = \frac{\| \tilde{C} \ket{\psi} \|_2^2}{(0.5 + 0.5)^2} = \frac{\| C \ket{\psi} \|_2^2}{\|C\|_2^2}
\end{equation*}
because both coefficients of our LCU were $0.5$.
We apply the same process for a normalized $\tilde{B}$.

Let $U_{\tilde{B}}$ and $U_{\tilde{C}}$ be the corresponding LCU operations. 
If we apply them sequentially, using two ancilla qubits, the resulting state works out as:
\begin{equation*}
    U_{\tilde{B}} U_{\tilde{C}} \ket{\psi}\ket{00} = (\tilde{B}\tilde{C}\ket{\psi})\ket{00} + \ket{\Phi^\perp} = \left( \frac{1}{\|B\|_2 \|C\|_2} BC \ket{\psi} \right)\ket{00} + \ket{\Phi^\perp}
\end{equation*}
where $\ket{\Phi^\perp}$ contains all throwaway states.
Naturally, the probability of measuring the desired $\ket{00}$ substate works out to be:

\begin{equation}
    P_{seq} = \left\| \frac{1}{\|B\|_2 \|C\|_2} BC \ket{\psi} \right\|_2^2 = \frac{\| BC \ket{\psi} \|_2^2}{\|B\|_2^2 \|C\|_2^2}
\end{equation}

Alternatively, when we merge the two operators, we normalize the merged operator $M = BC$ directly, obtaining $\tilde{M} = M / \|BC\|_2$.
The probability for the merged operator is then:
\begin{equation}
    P_{merged} = \| \tilde{M} \ket{\psi} \|_2^2 = \frac{\| BC \ket{\psi} \|_2^2}{\|BC\|_2^2}
\end{equation}

Notice how the numerator is identical, so we only need to compare the denominators.

The L2 norm is sub-multiplicative \cite{horn2012matrix}, meaning the following inequality holds:
\begin{equation*}
    \|BC\|_2 \le \|B\|_2 \|C\|_2
\end{equation*}

Because these norms are strictly positive scalars, squaring both sides and taking the inverse produces:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{\|BC\|_2^2} \ge \frac{1}{\|B\|_2^2 \|C\|_2^2} \implies P_{merged} \ge P_{seq}
\end{equation}


In order to quantify the improvement, we directly compared the merged and sequential approaches.
We applied both circuits (without the streaming) on the same initial states.
We computed the probability by looking at the total amplitude of the $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{00}$ substates respectively.
Additionally we also counted the total gates and circuit depths in the previously used IBM Eagle gate set.

\section{Experimental Validation}
\label{sec:bc-validation}

The quantum circuit was validated against the classical implementation mirroring the same boundary condition mechanism.

\paragraph*{1D Bounce-Back Boundary Conditions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Configuration:} D1Q3 with 32 lattice sites for 150 iterations.
    \item \textbf{Links:} $\{(-1),(0),(1)\}$ with weights $\{1/6, 2/3, 1/6\}$.
    \item \textbf{Initial condition:} Gaussian hill of amplitude 1.0 centered at $x=16$, width $\sigma=3$.
    \item \textbf{Velocity field:} $u=0.1$ (rightward) for half the iterations, then $u=0$ for the rest.
\end{itemize}
\paragraph*{Boundary conditions:}
Solid walls at sites 0 and $N-1$ with boundary conditions applied at adjacent fluid nodes (sites 1 and $N-2$). 
All fluid streaming into the wall gets redirected to the stationary link instead.

At site 1 (left wall):
\[f_{-1}^{post} = 0 \qquad f_{0}^{post} = f_{0}^{pre} + f_{-1}^{pre} \qquad f_{1}^{post} = f_{1}^{pre}\]
At site $N-2$ (right wall):
\[f_{-1}^{post} = f_{-1}^{pre} \qquad f_{0}^{post} = f_{0}^{pre} + f_{1}^{pre} \qquad f_{1}^{post} = 0\]

\paragraph*{2D Circle Obstacle}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Configuration:} D2Q5 with $8 \times 8$ lattice sites for 25 iterations.
    \item \textbf{Links:} $\{(0,0),(\pm1,0),(0,\pm1)\}$ with weights $\{1/3, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6\}$.
    \item \textbf{Initial condition:} Horizontal line of density $0.9$ at $y=1$, background density $0.05$.
    \item \textbf{Velocity field:} Constant upward push $v=0.15$.
\end{itemize}
\paragraph*{Boundary conditions:}
Central circular obstacle of radius $2$, occupying 12 lattice sites with boundary conditions applied at the 12 adjacent fluid nodes, redistributing outgoing links. The lattice margins themselves are periodic due to the default streaming wrapping.

The general idea of the boundary condition is to reflect $20\%$ of the outgoing fluid, then equally distribute the rest between the remaining "safe" links.
A link is considered safe if it does not stream into the wall. The rest link $(0,0)$ is always left unchanged.
There are two flavors of boundary nodes with slightly different conditions: edges and corners.

Consider the edge node at $(6, 4)$. The outgoing only link is $(-1,0)$, which will get split between $(+1,0)$ and the two tangential directions $(0,-1)$ and $(0,+1)$.
The full set of conditions is:
\begin{align*}
    f_{(-1,0)}^{post} &= 0 \\
    f_{(+1,0)}^{post}  &=  0.2\,f_{(-1,0)}^{pre} + f_{(+1,0)}^{pre} \\
    f_{(0,-1)}^{post} &=  0.4\,f_{(-1,0)}^{pre} + f_{(0,-1)}^{pre} \\
    f_{(0,+1)}^{post} &=  0.4\,f_{(-1,0)}^{pre} + f_{(0,+1)}^{pre} \\
    f_{(0,0)}^{post} &= f_{(0,0)}^{pre} 
\end{align*}

For the corner node at $(2, 3)$, both $(+1,0)$ and $(0,+1)$ point into the wall.
After reflecting, each outgoing link only has one remaining safe direction left: $(0,-1)$ and $(-1,0)$ respectively.
The full set of conditions is:
\begin{align*}
    f_{(+1,0)}^{post}   &= 0 \\
    f_{(0,+1)}^{post}   &= 0 \\
    f_{(-1,0)}^{post}  &= 0.2\, f_{(+1,0)}^{pre}  + f_{(-1,0)}^{pre} + 0.8\, f_{(0,+1)}^{pre} \\
    f_{(0,-1)}^{post}  &= 0.8\, f_{(+1,0)}^{pre} + f_{(0,-1)}^{pre} + 0.2\, f_{(0,+1)}^{pre} \\
    f_{(0,0)}^{post} &= f_{(0,0)}^{pre} 
\end{align*}

\section{Performance Impact}
\label{sec:bc-complexity}

For the classical preprocessing, the cost of performing SVD can be ignored as it is only done once before simulation starts, getting amortized over the iterations.

The circuit depth of the transpiled circuit does not change its complexity class, remaining at $\mathcal{O}(M\cdot N)$.
What the presence of the boundary condition operator does impact is how close to the worst case our matrix gets.
Because the operator is no longer diagonal, each unitary will have a significatly larger number of gates and circuit depth.

To quantify this impact, we extended the previous transpilation experiment to include variations with boundary conditions defined.
In both cases, boundary nodes were defined at the edge of the respective lattice.
