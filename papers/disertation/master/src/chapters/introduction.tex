\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chapter:intro}

Simulating physical quantum systems has been one of the original proposed applications of quantum computing \cite{feynman2018simulating}.
While quantum computing for quantum physics is natural and obvious, quantum computing for classical physics is often overlooked.
Complex physical systems, especially ones described by partial differential equations, suffer from high-dimensional phase spaces,
making large scale or high precision simulations difficult.
The simulation of fluids is one such class of problems.

\textbf{Computational Fluid Dynamics} (CFD) is the field concerned with the numerical simulation of fluids and their behavior.
It has a wide range of important applications such as aerodynamics, meteorology and medicine \cite{blazek2015computational}.
Over time, the scale, complexity and accuracy required from CFD applications have kept increasing.
Modern CFD simulations are computationally expensive and are often designed to run on supercomputer architectures \cite{karp2024experience}.
A quantum speedup for CFD simulations could have significant impact, assuming a future in which quantum computers are practical and available.

Among the various methods explored in the literature, the Quantum Lattice Boltzmann Method (QLBM) is a promising candidate with several groups actively researching it \cite{budinski2021quantum,itani2024quantum,kocherla2024fully, xu2025improved, tiwari2025algorithmic,wawrzyniak2025base}.
The QLBM, as the name suggests, is a variant of the classical Lattice Boltzmann Method for CFD designed to run on quantum computers.
The standard LBM steps: Collision, and Streaming are transformed into quantum gates and circuits with the goal of achieving better time and memory complexities via quantum parallelism and superposition.

In this thesis, I present a fully generic and parameterized implementation of a QLBM simulator with support for arbitraty velocity sets and in-lattice boundary conditions.
Previous research hardcoded specific boundary conditions, such as bounce-back, directly into the circuit.
My simulator allows for more flexibility by defining boundary conditions locally for each individual site. The only restrictions is that they must be linear and norm-preserving.
As of the time of writing, and to the best of my knowledge, this is the only such implementation available.
